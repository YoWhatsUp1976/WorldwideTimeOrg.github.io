LEAP SECONDS

WHAT ARE LEAP SECONDS?

A leap second is a one-second adjustment applied to Coordinated Universal Time (UTC) to keep it synchronized with Earth's rotation. Since Earth's rotation is gradually slowing, leap seconds are occasionally added to atomic time to maintain alignment with solar time.

LEAP HOURS

⚠ IMPORTANT NOTE: "Leap hours" are not an official timekeeping adjustment in atomic time standards. This tracker monitors Daylight Saving Time (DST) transitions, which create hour shifts in civil timekeeping.

WHAT ARE "LEAP HOURS"?

While not an official term in horology, "leap hours" commonly refers to Daylight Saving Time (DST) transitions where clocks "spring forward" (losing an hour) or "fall back" (gaining an hour). These are adjustments to civil time, not atomic time standards like UTC or TAI.

GLOBAL DST VARIATIONS

Not all countries observe Daylight Saving Time. Countries near the equator typically don't use DST. The European Union observes DST on different dates than North America (last Sunday in March and October). Some regions like Arizona (except Navajo Nation) and Hawaii in the US don't observe DST at all.

LEAP YEARS

WHAT ARE LEAP YEARS?

A leap year contains 366 days instead of the usual 365. An extra day (February 29) is added to keep the calendar year synchronized with the astronomical year. A year is a leap year if it's divisible by 4, except for century years which must be divisible by 400.

ATOMIC TIME

INTERNATIONAL ATOMIC TIME (TAI)

International Atomic Time (TAI) is a high-precision time standard based on the average of over 400 atomic clocks worldwide. Unlike UTC, TAI is not adjusted for leap seconds and provides a continuous time scale for scientific applications.

HOW ATOMIC CLOCKS WORK

Atomic clocks measure time based on the resonance frequency of atoms, typically cesium-133. One second is defined as 9,192,631,770 oscillations of the cesium atom's radiation. This provides the most accurate time measurement possible, forming the basis for UTC and global time synchronization.

ABOUT WTO

OUR MISSION

The Worldwide Time Organization is dedicated to providing accurate, atomic-synchronized time information to people across the globe. We maintain precise time standards and educate the public about timekeeping systems, leap seconds, and the science behind atomic time.

TIME STANDARDS & SYNCHRONIZATION

Our time displays synchronize with WorldTimeAPI.org, which uses NTP (Network Time Protocol) servers connected to atomic clock networks. The site automatically syncs every 5 minutes to maintain accuracy. All times follow international standards established by the International Bureau of Weights and Measures (BIPM) and the International Earth Rotation and Reference Systems Service (IERS).

ACCURACY & LIMITATIONS

While we sync with atomic time sources via NTP, browser-based applications have limitations. Network latency, device clock drift, and browser performance affect precision. For scientific applications requiring atomic-level accuracy, consult official atomic clock facilities like NIST or USNO directly.
